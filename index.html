Group A Streptococcus in the United States

Group A Streptococcus (GAS) causes a wide spectrum of disease—from mild pharyngitis to life-threatening invasive infections including necrotizing fasciitis and streptococcal toxic shock syndrome. This dashboard draws on CDC Active Bacterial Core (ABCs) surveillance data from 1997–2023.

National Trends

Invasive GAS Case and Death Rates, 1997–2023

Annual incidence and mortality rates (per 100,000 population) from CDC Active Bacterial Core (ABCs) national surveillance. Rates dropped sharply during the COVID-19 pandemic, then rebounded to record highs in 2022–2023.

Measure
About this chart
Rates are estimated from CDC Active Bacterial Core (ABCs) surveillance, a population-based active surveillance system that identifies all laboratory-confirmed cases of invasive GAS disease (GAS isolated from a normally sterile site such as blood, CSF, or pleural fluid). Rates are calculated per 100,000 population using U.S. Census intercensal and postcensal estimates. Death rates reflect in-hospital deaths among ABCs cases. National data cover all surveillance sites. ABCs surveillance covers approximately 15% of the U.S. population.

Clinical Features & Resistance

Syndrome Distribution and Antimicrobial Resistance

How the clinical presentation of invasive GAS has shifted over time, alongside trends in antimicrobial resistance among GAS isolates.

Syndrome distribution (% of national invasive GAS cases)
About this chart
Percentages represent the proportion of invasive GAS cases with each clinical syndrome recorded at the time of hospitalization. Cases may be assigned to more than one syndrome; totals may exceed 100%. STSS = streptococcal toxic shock syndrome; NF = necrotizing fasciitis. Cellulitis has increased substantially as a share of invasive GAS cases over the surveillance period.
Antimicrobial resistance (% isolates non-susceptible, national)
About this chart
Percent of invasive GAS isolates tested that were non-susceptible to each antibiotic. Data for specific antibiotics are only available from 2006 onward in this dataset. GAS remains universally susceptible to penicillin and vancomycin (not shown). Erythromycin and clindamycin resistance are clinically relevant for patients with penicillin allergy. Tetracycline resistance rose sharply through 2021.
Molecular Epidemiology

EMM Type Distribution, 1997–2023

The proportion of invasive GAS isolates belonging to key EMM types. EMM typing identifies the surface protein gene that determines immune escape, vaccine targets, and strain emergence. emm1 historically dominated; recent shifts show rising emm12 and declining emm89.

About this chart
EMM type percentages represent the share of isolates typed each year belonging to each EMM type. "Other" aggregates all remaining types. Null values indicate the type was not yet tracked or represented fewer than the reporting threshold for that year. The dramatic drop in emm1 and rise in emm12 in 2023 reflects an ongoing shift in the circulating strains following the COVID-19 pandemic-era disruption of GAS transmission dynamics.
Data Sources

About the Data

CDC Active Bacterial Core Surveillance (ABCs)
Population-based active laboratory surveillance for invasive bacterial pathogens across U.S. surveillance sites, covering approximately 15% of the U.S. population. Cases are defined by isolation of GAS from a normally sterile site (blood, CSF, pleural fluid, etc.). Annual data available 1997–2023. Includes demographic stratification, clinical syndrome, antimicrobial susceptibility, and EMM typing. Public domain — CDC data is not subject to copyright restrictions.
PopHIVE Ingest Pipeline
All data shown here were processed through the PopHIVE standardized ingest pipeline. Raw data were downloaded directly from CDC surveillance reports and transformed into a consistent format. Source code and processed data files are publicly available at the PopHIVE/Ingest GitHub repository under the gas branch. Data are updated annually following ABCs publication.

Note: This dashboard uses national aggregate estimates from ABCs surveillance. For clinical or policy decisions, consult the primary CDC surveillance reports.
